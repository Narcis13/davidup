Product Vision — pre-SaaS, founder-validated path

davidup is a deterministic JSON engine for motion. The SaaS turns it into a composable video platform — one where every brand, every behavior, every scene, every render compounds.

Today: a tiny, perfectly determinist motion-graphics engine driven by JSON, an MCP server, and a single-process renderer. Tomorrow: a multi-tenant platform where the things you make — a behavior, a template, a scene, a brand kit — become reusable currency. The platform isn't a video editor. It's a registry of motion that LLMs and humans both speak.

01 — Thesis

Why this product, why now, why davidup specifically.

The wedge

Three forces are colliding, and there's no product sitting at the intersection.

Programmatic video has always been bifurcated: code-first tools (Remotion, After Effects expressions, Lottie) demand developers; visual tools (Canva, Veed, CapCut) produce one-off content. Neither composes. Neither is LLM-native.

FORCE 01

LLMs author structured data, not pixels.

Claude can write 1,300 lines of clean composition JSON, but it can't manipulate a node-graph UI. The format is the interface. davidup's JSON-first design is already where the puck is going.

FORCE 02

Brand teams need variants, not videos.

Marketing wants "this 30-second explainer in 12 locales × 4 product lines × 3 aspect ratios." Per-pixel editors can't multiply. A composable engine with parameterized scenes can — at zero marginal effort.

FORCE 03

Determinism is becoming a feature.

AI-generated video is non-reproducible. Compliance, legal, and brand teams will pay for "same input, same pixels, byte-identical." Davidup's engine already guarantees it. That's a moat the generative crowd structurally can't have.

The opportunity isn't "another video editor." It's a registry of motion primitives where the unit of reuse is a $behavior, a $template, a scene, or a brand kit — and the rendering substrate is deterministic enough to trust with brand-critical output. Both humans and LLMs author against the same library, the same brand kit, the same render farm.

02 — Position

The category map. Where davidup sits, and what it competes with on each axis.

Category map

The quadrant nobody owns.

The competitive space splits cleanly on two axes: how content is authored (visual vs structured) and what's under the hood (generative vs deterministic). The valuable quadrant — structured + deterministic — is where teams will live, because it's where variants, compliance, and agent-driven authoring all converge.

structured / programmatic visual / WYSIWYG generative · stochastic deterministic · reproducible Sora / Runway Remotion Lottie Pictory / HeyGen Canva · Veed · CapCut After Effects davidup

davidup sits at the top-right: high in structure (JSON, $ref, behaviors, scenes — agent-friendly), high in determinism (skia + content-addressed library + locked versions). The visual editor (v1.0) doesn't move us left — it adds a surface on top of the same canonical JSON.

03 — Surface

The six product pillars. Each one stands alone; together they're the moat.

Product surface

Six pillars. Each compounds the others.

PILLAR 01

The Editor

Per-scene tabbed visual editor over the canonical JSON. Drag behaviors and templates from the library; everything is a deterministic compile away from a render-ready clip.

PILLAR 02

The Library

Behaviors, templates, scenes, assets, fonts, and brand kits — versioned, content-addressed, share-able. Private to a workspace, or published to the public marketplace.

PILLAR 03

The Brain

An in-editor AI assistant powered by the existing MCP server. Speaks JSON natively; can author, edit, validate, and render. The assistant uses the library you've built.

PILLAR 04

The Renderer

Managed render farm with deterministic guarantees, progress streaming, presets (MP4/WebM/GIF/PNG seq.), and parallelized variant rendering for locale × format × aspect-ratio matrices.

PILLAR 05

The Brand Kit

Locked colors, fonts, logos, motion language. Every template and scene inside a workspace inherits the kit. Brand teams enforce it; everyone else benefits from it.

PILLAR 06

The Distribution

Public/private/scheduled links, embed snippets, API for video-on-demand. Render-by-URL with parameter overrides — perfect for personalized ads, transactional emails, paid-ads variants.

04 — The Library

The heart of the platform. The thing that turns "I used davidup once" into "I can't leave davidup."

The library is already specified architecturally — §12 of COMPOSITION_PRIMITIVES.md defines content-addressed library files with behaviors, templates, scenes, and assets. The SaaS makes it discoverable, versioned, and shareable.

A dynamic, content-addressed, growing registry

Every artifact a user creates becomes platform fuel.

Six kinds of artifact, each first-class in storage, each with a search, a preview, a version history, a visibility scope (private workspace → org → public), and a permalink. The library grows organically — what gets made gets reused.

Type 01 · primitivesBehaviors
Motion verbs — one-shot, parameterized animations attached to existing items. popIn, shake, kenBurns, typewriter, parallax. Authored as JSON tween-generators with named params. // preview: 1-second animated thumbnail · auto-generated
Type 02 · primitivesTemplates
Parameterized scene-fragments — a titleCard, a lowerThird, a bulletList, a captionBurst. They emit items + tweens (often via behaviors). Each template ships a JSON schema for its params. // preview: a 4-frame strip across param defaults
Type 03 · primitivesScenes
Full reusable sequences with internal items, tweens, assets — the unit of "drop a 5-second product reveal here." Sealed-internal-tweens guarantee no leakage with the host composition. // preview: a real 1-frame-per-second flipbook
Type 04 · assetsImages, video, audio
Versioned, deduped by content hash. Searchable by tags, color, dominant subject (auto-extracted). Hot-swap by id when you re-upload a corrected logo and 800 compositions update at once. // supports image/png · jpg · webp · mp3 · wav · mp4 · webm
Type 05 · typographyFonts
Self-hosted or licensed-account fonts (Adobe Fonts, Google Fonts, custom uploads). Subsetted on render to keep clip sizes tight. Pairings ("display + body") suggested at template-bind time. // every font has provenance, license, and a usage receipt
Type 06 · constraintsBrand kits
A bundle: colors, fonts, motion language ("our pop-in feels like easeOutBack(1.7), never linear"), logo lockups, lower-third style. Every template inside a workspace binds to the kit by default. Override is explicit and surfaced. // the brand kit is the contract; the renderer enforces it

Why this is the moat: every artifact a workspace creates is content-addressed and reusable. After three months of usage, a team has built up a private library that's impossible to replicate elsewhere — and the AI assistant has learned their motion vocabulary. Churning is no longer "I'll switch tools." It's "I'll throw away two years of accumulated motion language." That's the lock-in flywheel.

A · LIBRARY

Drag-to-compose, never drag-to-pixel.

Templates, behaviors, and scenes are first-class draggables. Dropping a template emits a typed instance with the brand kit pre-bound — never a bag of raw items.

B · STAGE

The preview is the production engine.

Browser preview runs attach(), the same engine path as the server renderer. No "looks fine, renders wrong" drift. Source-map "Reveal" maps every pixel back to its authoring line.

C · INSPECTOR

Typed params. Surfaced overrides.

Every field knows its schema. Overridden params (orange dot) are visually distinct from inherited defaults — so brand violations are obvious, never hidden.

D · TIMELINE

Bars are semantic, not numeric.

Color codes mean something: templates, behaviors, scenes. Sealed scenes can't be edited internally from this timeline — you tab into them.

05 — The Brain

The MCP server already exists. The SaaS exposes it as an in-editor co-pilot that knows your library, your brand, your workspace.

AI co-pilot, MCP-native

Not a "video AI." A motion peer that authors against your library.

The hard problem with AI video is that pixel-level generation is non-reproducible and non-brand-safe. Davidup's brain doesn't generate pixels. It generates composition JSON — using your templates, your behaviors, your brand kit. Every output is a deterministic compile away from a video.

CAPABILITY

Authors from intent.

"Make a 30-second product reveal in our brand using the kenburnsImage template, with three feature callouts." → returns a valid scene, ready to preview.

CAPABILITY

Edits in place.

"Slow down the popIn by 30%, push it back 0.5s." → modifies the current selection's tweens. Atomic, validatable, reversible (Cmd-Z).

CAPABILITY

Generates variants.

"Same scene in 9:16, 1:1, and 16:9; English, Spanish, Portuguese; with and without the logo lockup." → 18 valid compositions, queued.

CAPABILITY

Learns your library.

The brain is grounded in your workspace's library. It will not invent a behavior that doesn't exist; if it wants parallax and you don't have it, it asks to install @davidup/effects-classic.

CAPABILITY

Enforces brand.

The brand kit is a hard constraint at generation time. The model literally cannot emit colors or fonts outside the kit unless the user explicitly opts out — and that opt-out is logged.

CAPABILITY

Explains itself.

Every AI-authored change ships with a one-line rationale and a diff. "Reveal source ⌘J" jumps from rendered pixel → tween → reason. Auditable AI is the only AI brand teams will tolerate.

Flywheel

The library is the loop.

Each turn of the wheel makes the next turn cheaper. The platform isn't "use it once and forget"; it's a compounding asset. The longer a workspace lives, the harder it gets to leave — not because of lock-in friction, but because their motion vocabulary is their library.

Risks worth naming

Five things that could kill this.

R1 — Editor UX is hard.

"Per-scene tab UI over canonical JSON" sounds clean but the timeline / overrides / source-map nav represents 6+ months of design work. After Effects has 25 years of polish here.

Mitigation Don't try to beat AE on depth. Win on composability and AI authoring; ship a deliberately minimal editor that's perfect for the "drop scene, tweak params" flow.

R2 — Render-cost economics.

Skia + ffmpeg at 4K 60fps eats CPU. If average user renders 300 minutes/month at cost-per-minute > price, the unit economics invert.

Mitigation Tiered render priority (slow free tier ≈ amortized cost); GPU encode for high tiers; aggressive frame caching on unchanged scenes (determinism makes this trivial).

R3 — Remotion is doing this.

Remotion has the JSX engine, has Lambda renders, has traction. They could ship a visual editor and a brand kit in a quarter.

Mitigation They're React-first; that's a wall, not a bridge, for non-devs and LLMs. The JSON-native + library + MCP-native combo is a structurally different position. Don't chase, differentiate.

R4 — Generative video eats the market.

Sora 3 / Veo 4 / Runway gen-N could make all motion graphics feel quaint by 2027.

Mitigation Hybrid is the answer: generated b-roll lives inside a davidup scene as an asset. The deterministic compositing layer doesn't compete with generation — it absorbs it.

R5 — Marketplace cold-start.

A registry with 12 templates isn't differentiated. A registry with 1,200 is. Getting from 12 → 1,200 without paying creators is the standard marketplace problem.

Mitigation Seed it yourself in v1.x dogfooding. Open paid publishing only once you cross 200 paying workspaces (each one is a built-in audience). Don't open marketplace early.

R6 — Solo founder bandwidth.

The thing you're describing is 18 months of full-time engineering. You're one person and the engine still has rough edges (skia-canvas codec issues, etc).

Mitigation Strict scope. Validate Stage 1 alone. Hire #1 only after Studio tier shows $10k MRR. Use Claude as a co-engineer — you already do.

Founder advice — read this last

Before you build any of this — validate these five things.

You said you want to validate for your own use cases before going SaaS. Good instinct. Here's the order that matters — each one gates the next:

Five experiments. Each one a week. Each one un-skippable.

  1. Experiment 1 — Use it for one real project of yours. Pick a real need: a launch video, a personal portfolio reel, an investor update. If davidup doesn't beat your current tool for that one job, nothing else matters yet. Document what hurts.
  2. Experiment 2 — Build one brand kit and three scenes for yourself. Do you actually reuse them? Or does each new project want bespoke motion? If the latter, the library thesis is wrong — and you should learn that before building marketplace infra.
  3. Experiment 3 — Have Claude (via MCP) author three real videos. Not toy demos. Real briefs. Where does the AI fail? Missing primitives? Missing brand context? That's your v1.2 backlog, prioritized.
  4. Experiment 4 — Show three target-buyer profiles a 90-second demo. One indie creator. One in-house brand designer. One agency PM. Don't pitch. Show. Watch where their eyes go. If nobody asks "can I try it?" — your pitch is off, not the product.
  5. Experiment 5 — Charge one person $50 to render one project for them. Manual delivery is fine. The signal you want is "did money change hands for this specific output?" — not "is the demo cool?" Cool demos don't pay rent.

Only after all five are green should you start writing the multi-tenant auth, the billing, and the render queue. The platform vision is correct, in my view — but the danger for any founder is building Stage 2 before Stage 1 has been honestly tested. The good news: the engine you have today is already enough to run all five experiments. Don't build anything new until Experiment 1 ships.
